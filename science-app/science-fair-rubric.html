What Is a Science Fair Project?

A science fair project is your chance to explore something you are curious about and think like a scientist. Every project begins with one important question‚Äîa question that matters to you and makes you wonder, "Why does this happen?" or "What would happen if‚Ä¶?" A good science fair question is clear, focused, and something you can test or investigate.

After choosing your question, you will research what other scientists have already learned about your topic. This is called background research or a literature review, and it helps you understand your topic better and plan a smart experiment. You will use this research to make a hypothesis, which is your best guess about what you think will happen and why. Then, you will design and carry out an experiment, collect and analyze data, and explain what your results mean.

Throughout the project, remember: this is your work, your thinking, and your learning journey as a scientist.

1) Project Title Ask a question (framing the focus) Brainstorm a clear, specific title that matches the research question; check spelling and clarity. Writing the title for your child or making it catchy but misleading.

Picking the project idea; completing internet searches; printing articles; writing the hypothesis for the student.

4) Results Test the hypothesis (record data/observations) Ask how data will be organized; check labels, units, and axes. Creating spreadsheets or graphs for the student (even if they type the data).
5) Analysis / Interpretation Analyze data and draw conclusions Prompt thinking with questions: "What patterns do you see? How does this compare to your research? What errors may have affected results?" Stating or writing the analysis for the student.

7) References Do background research (credit sources) Show how to cite sources; discuss credibility; remind that all facts and figures must be cited. Writing the bibliography or adding unused sources.
Final Display / Presentation Communicate results Be the audience for practice; help transport the display; check readability and organization. Writing any text on the board/slides; choosing graphics or color schemes.
